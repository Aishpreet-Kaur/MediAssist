MEDICAL KNOWLEDGE BASE - WOMEN'S HEALTH & NUTRITION
====================================================

MENSTRUAL CRAMPS (DYSMENORRHEA)
--------------------------------
Symptoms: Cramping lower abdominal pain before/during menstruation, lower back pain, nausea, headache, fatigue, diarrhea or constipation during period.

Types:
- Primary: No underlying condition — hormonal (prostaglandins).
- Secondary: Due to conditions like endometriosis, fibroids, PCOS.

Home Remedies:
1. Heat therapy — hot water bag on lower abdomen or back (most effective home remedy).
2. Gentle yoga poses: child's pose, cat-cow, supine twist.
3. Ginger tea — reduces prostaglandins, as effective as ibuprofen in some studies.
4. Turmeric milk — anti-inflammatory.
5. Fennel seeds (saunf) boiled in water — antispasmodic effect.
6. Light exercise: walking, yoga — releases endorphins.
7. Stay hydrated and reduce salt intake (reduces bloating).
8. Avoid caffeine and alcohol during period.
9. Magnesium-rich foods: dark chocolate, nuts, seeds, leafy greens.
10. Omega-3 fatty acids reduce inflammation.

OTC Medicines:
- Ibuprofen (400mg) — start 1-2 days before expected period for best results.
- Mefenamic acid (Meftal Spas) — reduces cramps and prostaglandins.
- Naproxen (500mg) — longer acting NSAID for cramps.
- Antispasmodic: Cyclopam, Buscopan for spasms.

When to See a Doctor:
- Severe cramps preventing normal daily activity.
- Pain worsening over months/years (may indicate endometriosis).
- Irregular periods, very heavy bleeding (>7 days), or bleeding between periods.
- Cramps not relieved by OTC medicines.
- Fever with pelvic pain.

POLYCYSTIC OVARY SYNDROME (PCOS)
----------------------------------
Symptoms: Irregular or absent periods, excess facial/body hair (hirsutism), acne, weight gain (especially belly), hair thinning on scalp, difficulty getting pregnant, skin tags, dark patches (acanthosis nigricans).

Home Management:
1. Weight loss — even 5-10% weight reduction restores menstrual regularity in many women.
2. Low-GI diet — reduces insulin resistance (the core issue in PCOS).
   - Eat: whole grains, legumes, vegetables, lean protein, healthy fats.
   - Avoid: white rice, sugar, processed foods, sugary drinks.
3. Exercise — cardio + strength training reduces insulin resistance.
4. Spearmint tea (2 cups/day) — reduces androgen levels, helps with hirsutism.
5. Cinnamon supplementation — improves insulin sensitivity.
6. Manage stress — cortisol worsens hormonal imbalance.
7. Sleep 7-9 hours — sleep deprivation worsens insulin resistance.
8. Inositol (Myo-inositol + D-chiro-inositol) — OTC supplement, strong evidence for PCOS.

When to See a Doctor:
- Irregular periods (less than 8 per year).
- Trying to conceive with PCOS.
- Severe acne or hirsutism.
- Suspicion of diabetes or metabolic syndrome alongside PCOS.

URINARY TRACT INFECTION (UTI)
------------------------------
Symptoms: Burning sensation when urinating, frequent urge to urinate (little comes out), cloudy, dark, or foul-smelling urine, pelvic pain (women), lower abdominal pressure.

Home Remedies (MILD early UTI only):
1. Drink 2-3 litres of water daily — flushes bacteria.
2. Unsweetened cranberry juice — may prevent bacteria from adhering to bladder wall.
3. D-mannose supplement — evidence-based prevention and mild treatment.
4. Avoid caffeine, alcohol, spicy food — irritate the bladder.
5. Urinate frequently — don't hold urine.
6. Urinate after sexual intercourse.
7. Wear cotton underwear, avoid tight clothing.
8. Warm compress on lower abdomen for pain relief.

OTC Medicines:
- Phenazopyridine (Pyridium) — urinary analgesic, relieves burning (turns urine orange — normal).
- Antibiotics REQUIRE prescription (Nitrofurantoin, Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole).

When to See a Doctor:
- Symptoms not improving within 48 hours.
- ANY UTI symptoms in men, children, elderly, or pregnant women — see doctor immediately.
- Upper UTI / Kidney infection signs: fever, chills, back/flank pain, nausea (URGENT).
- Recurring UTIs (more than 2-3 per year).
- Blood in urine.

ANAEMIA (IRON DEFICIENCY)
--------------------------
Symptoms: Fatigue, weakness, pale skin, cold hands and feet, dizziness, headache, shortness of breath, brittle nails, unusual cravings (pica — craving ice, dirt, starch), rapid heartbeat.

Extremely common in Indian women — affects 50%+ of women of reproductive age.

Home Remedies:
1. Iron-rich foods:
   - Non-heme (plant): Spinach, methi, rajma, chana, lentils, tofu, pumpkin seeds, jaggery.
   - Heme (animal): Chicken, fish, eggs (more bioavailable).
2. Vitamin C with iron-rich meals enhances absorption (lemon juice on dal, amla).
3. Avoid tea/coffee with meals — tannins reduce iron absorption.
4. Cook in iron cookware — increases iron content of food.
5. Soaked and sprouted legumes — improve iron bioavailability.
6. Dates and raisins — good iron sources for snacking.
7. Beetroot juice — rich in folate, iron, supports haemoglobin.

OTC Supplements:
- Ferrous sulphate / Ferrous fumarate — take on empty stomach or with vitamin C.
- Combination Iron + Folic Acid tablets (Dexorange, Autrin).

When to See a Doctor:
- Haemoglobin below 8 g/dL — moderate to severe anaemia needs medical management.
- Symptoms severely affecting daily functioning.
- Anaemia not responding to iron supplements after 1 month.
- Iron deficiency with rectal bleeding (rule out GI source of blood loss).

VITAMIN D DEFICIENCY
---------------------
Symptoms: Fatigue, bone and muscle pain, frequent infections, depression, hair loss, impaired wound healing. Often ASYMPTOMATIC.

Extremely common in India despite abundant sunshine — due to indoor lifestyle, skin pigmentation, clothing.

Normal Levels: 30-100 ng/mL. Deficiency: <20 ng/mL.

Home Remedies:
1. Safe sun exposure — 10-20 mins between 10am-2pm, arms and legs exposed, without sunscreen.
2. Fatty fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel), egg yolks, mushrooms exposed to sunlight.
3. Fortified foods: milk, cereals, orange juice.

OTC Supplements:
- Vitamin D3 (Cholecalciferol): 1000-2000 IU daily for maintenance.
- For correction of deficiency: 60,000 IU weekly for 8-12 weeks (sachet form: Uprise D3, Calcirol) — but confirm dosage with doctor.
- Take with a fatty meal for best absorption.
- Combine with Vitamin K2 (MK-7) for bone health.

When to See a Doctor:
- Confirmed deficiency by blood test (25-OH Vitamin D).
- Severe deficiency (<12 ng/mL).
- Bone pain, muscle weakness, or fracture with low trauma.
